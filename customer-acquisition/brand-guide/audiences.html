"I've been watching this neighborhood for months. I know the price history. I know the school ratings. And I still need to call an agent just to see the place — and they'll expect a commission for it."
94% of buyers do their own research online before engaging anyone. The frustration isn't information — it's access. Traditional agents gatekeep showing access and then charge 2.5–3% for what the buyer already knows. The emotional trigger: they feel capable but constrained.

The chaperone model is the unlock — especially for relocators who can't fly to Tampa every weekend. The AI layer handles the thing buyers fear most: am I offering the right number? The Sage archetype dominates here — showing the comp logic, the negotiation rationale, the offer strategy transparently.

Concierge Hero Journey
From DoorDash driver to
home showing specialist
1
Act 1 · The Gig Ceiling
The vehicle math doesn't work. The dignity math is worse.
"After gas, wear, and time — DoorDash isn't $18/hr. It's closer to $11. And I can't tell anyone at a party what I do without the conversation dying."
The gig worker's pain is two-layered: the real earnings math (vehicle depreciation eats more than most calculate) and the identity cost (no one introduces themselves as "a food delivery person" with pride). Both are resolvable. Address both.
Stuck Under-earning Undignified
2
Act 2 · The Discovery
$20 per showing. 40 minutes. Their own neighborhood.
"Wait — I'm already driving around Tampa. I know these streets. I can get $20 to let someone into a house for 40 minutes? In daylight? In a clean house? And they call me a home showing specialist?"
The hook must run the numbers instantly. A 40-minute showing at $20 = $30/hr effective. Two showings a day, five days = $200/week, $800/month supplemental. The earning potential visualization must be immediate and concrete. Address the scam concern upfront — background check, app, platform-backed, insurance covered.
Surprise Skepticism → legitimacy check Excitement
3
Act 3 · The Upgrade
Earning more. Wearing less. Proud of the title.
"I did 11 showings last week — $220. My car barely moved. I met a family from New York buying in Westchase and told them everything about the flood zones, the school ratings, the new Publix going in. They messaged me afterward to say thank you. I'm a home showing specialist now."
The transformation is financial AND identity. "Home showing specialist" is a real title they can say out loud. The local knowledge angle — the thing they've always had but never monetized — becomes their professional superpower.

Top Objections + Responses
Objection
"$20 per showing seems low."
Response
A 40-minute showing at $20 is $30/hr effective. Two showings a day is $40. Five days = $200/week, $800/month. DoorDash after real costs is $11–15/hr. The math isn't close — and your car doesn't move much.
Objection
"What if something goes wrong? Am I liable?"
Response
Platform insurance covers property incidents during showings. Your role is to facilitate access — not advise on the purchase. Clear scripting tells you exactly what to say and not say. You're covered.
Objection
"What if buyers ask questions I can't answer?"
Response
"That's a great question — let me connect you to our AI advisor for that." That's the script. You're a showing specialist, not a real estate advisor. Your job is the door, the tour, and your neighborhood knowledge. The AI handles the rest.
Objection
"How steady is the demand? Will there be enough showings?"
Response
Tampa Bay saw 3,452 home sales in July 2025 alone. For every sale, there are 5–10 showings. That's 17,000–35,000 showing events per month in Tampa Bay. We have more demand than chaperones right now.
